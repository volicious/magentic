# Decision Authority Standard

## Purpose

This document defines decision ownership across the organization.

Its purpose is to:

- Eliminate decision ambiguity.
- Clarify accountability.
- Prevent role overlap.
- Improve decision quality.
- Ensure decisions are made by the appropriate role.

Decision ownership defines who has the authority to make a decision.

Responsibility for gathering information or making recommendations does not imply decision-making authority.

---

# Guiding Principles

## Principle 1 — Decisions Have Owners

Every significant decision must have one clearly identified owner.

Multiple people may contribute information.

Only one role owns the final decision.

---

## Principle 2 — Recommendations Are Not Decisions

Many roles investigate, analyze, estimate, or recommend.

Those activities support decisions.

They do not replace decision authority.

---

## Principle 3 — Decisions Should Be Made at the Lowest Appropriate Level

Whenever practical, decisions should be made by the role closest to the work while remaining accountable for the outcome.

Escalate only when:

- Cross-functional authority is required.
- Budget approval is required.
- Organizational priorities conflict.
- Strategic direction is affected.

---

## Principle 4 — Decisions Should Be Evidence-Based

Decisions should be informed by:

- Business objectives
- Requirements
- Risks
- Dependencies
- Technical feasibility
- Customer impact
- Operational impact

Opinions alone are insufficient.

---

## Principle 5 — Document Significant Decisions

Significant decisions should include:

- Decision
- Decision owner
- Date
- Alternatives considered
- Rationale
- Business impact
- Related artifacts

# Escalation Guidelines

Escalate decisions when:

- Multiple decision owners disagree.
- Budget is affected.
- Strategic priorities conflict.
- Customer commitments change.
- Organizational standards conflict.
- Regulatory obligations exist.

Escalation should include:

- Decision required
- Decision owner
- Supporting analysis
- Options considered
- Risks
- Recommendation

---

# Decision Documentation

Significant decisions should be recorded in a Decision Log.

Each entry should include:

- Decision ID
- Date
- Decision Owner
- Contributors
- Description
- Alternatives Considered
- Rationale
- Business Impact
- Related Requirements
- Related User Stories
- Related Risks

---

# Responsibility vs. Authority

Understanding the distinction between responsibility and authority is critical.

A role may be responsible for:

- Research
- Analysis
- Documentation
- Recommendations

Another role may retain authority to make the final decision.

Example:

The Business Analyst documents acceptance criteria.

The Product Manager decides whether the feature should exist.

Engineering decides how to implement it.

---

# Decision Review

Before making a significant decision, verify:

- Is the decision owner clearly identified?
- Have the appropriate stakeholders been consulted?
- Have risks been documented?
- Have alternatives been considered?
- Is sufficient evidence available?
- Does the decision align with organizational standards?
- Has the decision been documented?

---

# Anti-Patterns

Avoid:

- Decisions by committee.
- Multiple owners for the same decision.
- Undocumented decisions.
- Escalating decisions that can be made locally.
- Making decisions outside assigned authority.
- Confusing recommendations with approvals.
- Revisiting decisions without new information.

---

# Guiding Principle

The best decisions are made by the person accountable for the outcome, informed by the people closest to the work.

Authority without information leads to poor decisions.

Information without authority leads to frustration.

The organization is most effective when every decision has one accountable owner and every owner receives complete, objective, and timely information before deciding.
